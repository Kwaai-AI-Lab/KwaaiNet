### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) was an organization founded in 1943 that aimed to unite African, Coloured, and Indian people against apartheid and colonialism. According to source [1], its programme was based on the Ten Point plan for equal rights. The NEUM adopted a boycott and non-collaboration policy - refusing to participate in any apartheid government institutions.

The organization played a significant role in revolutionizing the political scene with its doctrine of non-collaboration and the boycott weapon (source [7]). It galvanized the non-white disenfranchised to realize the need for a programme-based struggle that rejected old tactics of deputations and appeals for justice at round table conferences.

The NEUM was affiliated with other organizations, including the All African Convention, and had members who were intellectuals - individuals who were part of the movement but may not have contributed significantly in literature (source [14]). The organization's history is highlighted as significant in the struggle for liberation in South Africa, though its ideals were not fully achieved by 1956 (source [13]).

The NEUM was active in various regions, including the Transkeian Territories, where it received support from peasants who turned to the movement due to the regime's inability to provide solutions to their problems (source [8]).

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act [4, 5] was a law that transformed the country into a patchwork of Black “Coloured”, Indian, and White areas [4]. It was a key piece of legislation in the Apartheid regime's plans to preserve White rule for the next three hundred years [8]. The Act forced people to live in designated areas based on their racial classification.

The Group Areas Act affected District Six by declaring it a White area in 1966 [1], despite its history as a vibrant, multicultural community. As a result of this declaration, the Apartheid government carried out forced removals of all non-White residents to the Cape Flats [1]. The Group Areas Board's valuation of properties was generally below market value, forcing Coloured owners to accept paltry offers from prospective White purchasers [2, 5].

The Act also had a significant impact on the community's economy. The forced removal of residents led to the destruction of a thriving community and its way of life. Many people lost their homes, businesses, and livelihoods as a result of the Group Areas Act.

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?

The Unity Movement's boycott policy involved refusing to participate in any apartheid government institutions, including the Coloured Advisory Council [1]. They also instructed their members to spoil their votes by writing "For Full Democratic Rights" across the ballot slip if they were asked to vote in elections [19].

In addition, the movement encouraged its members to boycott the Group Areas Act, although some members who owned property were unable or unwilling to comply with this directive [10].
