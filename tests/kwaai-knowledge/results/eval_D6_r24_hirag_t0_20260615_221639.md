### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) was a South African political movement founded in 1943 that united non-European political organisations opposed to apartheid and racial discrimination. It advocated for non-collaboration with apartheid institutions and was closely connected to the All African Convention and the Teachers League of South Africa [1].

According to the sources, the NEUM brought the principle of non-Collaboration, the refutation of “race”, and the ideal of a non-racial society to the struggle for liberation [10]. The movement's doctrine of non-collaboration and the boycott weapon revolutionised the political scene, galvanising the non-white disenfranchised to a realisation of the need for a programme-based struggle that rejected the old tactics of deputations and appeals for justice at round table conferences [12].

The NEUM was led by young Turks, including Grandpa's daughter, Aunt Jane, and her brother, Dr. Goolam, who were moving spirits in the movement [9]. The movement also had notable contributions from individuals such as Victor Walter Wesley Wessels, whose brain was described as "needle sharp" and his invective as "utterly withering" [2].

The NEUM's ten-point programme included demands for equal education, free and equal education to the age of sixteen, and the full unfettered franchise - the right to elect and be elected to all the councils of the state [18].

The Group Areas Act had a devastating impact on District Six, as it led to the forced removals of thousands of people from their homes in the area. According to [12], the Act introduced separate group areas for different communities, which created a most vexing problem for families like those living in District Six, where some members were classified as Indian and others as Malay.

The Act also resulted in the destruction of the community's way of life, as people were forced to leave their homes and move to other areas. According to [14], by 1950, the Juggernaut of Apartheid was confidently grinding onwards, ploughing under the few remaining rights of the non-White oppressed.

The Group Areas Act had a significant impact on the residents of District Six, who were forced to adapt to new living conditions and struggle for their rights. The Act also led to the loss of municipal franchise for many residents, as they were moved to sub-economic houses in other areas.

Overall, the Group Areas Act was a key component of the Apartheid legislation, which aimed to preserve White rule and create separate group areas for different communities. Its impact on District Six was devastating, leading to forced removals, destruction of community life, and loss of rights for its residents.
