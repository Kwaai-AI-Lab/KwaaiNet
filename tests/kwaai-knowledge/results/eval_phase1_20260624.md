### q05 — Who was J.M.H. Gool?

J.M.H. Gool, whose full name is not provided in the sources, was a prosperous spice merchant of Pathan origin from the Province of Swat (via Rander in Gujarat, India) [1]. He arrived at the Cape via Mauritius in 1884 and established J.M.H. Gool & Co. [1]. He had two wives: Bibi Gool (mother of Ayesha, Mohamed Saaid, and Zohra) and Wahida Gool (mother of Abdul Hamid, Goolam, Jane, Fatima, Mariam, Zobeida, and Mohammed Hanief) [1].

He was a member of the community in Cape Town and interacted with notable figures such as Mahatma Gandhi, Cecil Rhodes, the Prince of Wales, George Bernard Shaw, and Dr. Abdulla Abdurahman [1]. He was also known to have attended political meetings alongside his acquaintances [4], and he shared business interests with a man named J.M. Wilson [9].

J.M.H. Gool is described as having been brought up in the British justice system and being an Oriental trader [14]. He founded the Hanaffi Quwatul Islam Mosque and built a grand mansion at 7 Buitencingle Street, where he lived with his family [1].

### q06 — Tell me about Buitencingle.

According to the sources, Buitencingle was a street in Cape Town where Yousuf (Joe) Rassool's grandfather, Haji Joosub Maulvi Hamid Gooli, lived in a 13-roomed mansion at number 7. The area was described as being wide and cobbled with traffic islands in the middle, sporting a few stunted palm trees. Opposite it stood the German Lutheran Church, which was grey, austere, and remote.

Buitencingle was bounded by Long Street, Buitencingle, and Loop Street, which branched off at right angles from Buitencingle to meander down to Waterkant Street a short distance from the waterfront of Rogge Bay. The Hanaffi Quwatul Islam Mosque, an outpost of Islam in an area becoming increasingly European, Jewish, and gentile, was located in Loop Street.

The Glen, a sylvan glen in Kloof Nek, was a place of haunting beauty that Yousuf (Joe) Rassool often visited as a child. It had a wooded vale with a babbling brook, steps winding down to a pathway, and a varied, dense green canopy of leaves.

Buitencingle was also described as the outermost belt of Cape Town at the time, beyond which lay the foothills of Table Mountain reached via Kloof Street.

Some notable events associated with Buitencingle include:

* The construction of the Hanaffi Quwatul Islam Mosque in 1898.
* Gandhi's visits to Buitencingle, where he would meet with Yousuf (Joe) Rassool's grandfather and other notables.
* The decline of the area as Whites, who owned businesses in Caledon Street, moved out due to the influx of Blacks and Coloureds.

The Gool mansion at 7 Buitencingle was a significant landmark, but it no longer exists. However, its history is still remembered by Yousuf (Joe) Rassool and others as an important part of Cape Town's past.

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

According to [2], the Group Areas Act was a piece of legislation that divided South Africa into separate areas for different racial groups.

The Group Areas Act had a significant impact on District Six, which was a thriving cosmopolitan area in the heart of Cape Town. According to various sources, including [1], [4], and [16], the Group Areas Act led to the dispersal of people from District Six to other areas, such as the Cape Flats.

The act effectively disfranchised many residents of District Six, as they were forced to vacate their homes due to poverty traps created by the legislation. According to [14] and [15], once a family's income rose above the sub-economic level, they had no choice but to leave their homes, even if it meant losing their municipal franchise.

The Group Areas Act also led to the decline of District Six as a community. As shops in Hanover Street started closing, the area began to die in the early 1950s [16]. The once-vibrant community scattered and disintegrated due to Apartheid policies.

Furthermore, according to [17], the Group Areas Act caused house prices to plummet when areas were declared White, as new buyers would only purchase at low prices. Additionally, sellers had to surrender half of their profits to the Group Area Boards.

The Group Areas Act was a key component of the Apartheid regime's plan to preserve white dominance and segregate communities along racial lines. Its impact on District Six was devastating, leading to the displacement of many residents and the erasure of the area's cultural identity.
