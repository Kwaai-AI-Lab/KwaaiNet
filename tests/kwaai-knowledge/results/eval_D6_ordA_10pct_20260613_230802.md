### q14 — Where was District Six and what kind of place was it?

District Six was a "vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighbourhood" [1] in Cape Town, South Africa. The author describes it as having "more life, more zest, more fire, more warmth and care than many other places one could mention." [20] It was a place where people of different cultures and backgrounds lived together and interacted with each other.

Initially, District Six had economic housing schemes for the affluent, but later, sub-economic schemes were built to entice people away from District Six. Many of the residents lost their municipal franchise because they lived in sub-economic houses [3].

In its heyday, District Six was a thriving cosmopolitan area, full of life and energy. It had a rich history of political struggle against apartheid, and the community was not defined by silence and acquiescence [7]. The author's grandfather, who was part of this community, was Adam Gool, the President of the British Indian League [10].

The place was known for its colorful characters, including street performers [14] and people selling penny sweets. It had a lively atmosphere, with people from different backgrounds living together and interacting with each other.

District Six was eventually demolished by the apartheid government in the 1970s and 1980s, and its residents were forcibly removed to make way for white-only developments. The author's account is an attempt to preserve the memory of this vibrant community and tell its story.

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

According to [14] and [20], the Group Areas Act was part of the Apartheid legislation introduced in South Africa. It classified people into different ethnic groups (Coloured, Indian, White) and forcibly relocated them to specific areas based on their group affiliation.

District Six was a thriving cosmopolitan area in Cape Town before the Group Areas Act was enforced, but it was eventually dispersed under this law [20]. The Act led to the uprooting of communities and ways of life, legitimizing poverty and oppression for the majority of its peoples [20].

A community of South African Indians was particularly aggrieved by this legislation, as they were compelled to live in their own restricted area outside the city [15]. This attempt at segregation affected people's lives drastically, transforming the country into a patchwork of Black "Coloured", Indian and White areas under Apartheid rule [14].

When implemented, the law had severe consequences for the property owners within the area. The Group Areas Board undervalued properties in District Six when compared to market value, forcing Coloured owners to accept paltry offers from prospective White buyers [19]. In addition, if an owner sold their property at a profit, half of it would have to go to the Board.

The implementation of this law led to the gradual decline and eventual dispersal of District Six's community, transforming the area into a symbol of Apartheid's devastating effects on South African society.

### q39 — What was District Six like as a neighbourhood before the forced removals?

According to [1] and [4], District Six was a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighborhood of Cape Town, South Africa. It was home to many residents from different racial backgrounds, including the Rassool and Gool families. The area was thriving with life, characterized by zest, warmth, and care.

[2] describes how apartheid legitimized poverty and oppression for the majority of its peoples, uprooting communities and ways of life. However, this passage does not directly describe what District Six was like as a neighborhood.

In [4], the author notes that to have been part of that pulsing life in District Six was an unforgettable education and privilege, adding to the richness of life. He recalls walking through the streets with his ten-year-old eyes, observing the community's joy of life.

Later in [15], when describing a poem about a classroom setup, it is written: "This map becomes their window and these windows / That open on their lives like crouching tombs" indicating that there was a sense of vitality to the area.

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?

According to excerpt [10], the Unity Movement's boycott policy was not clearly defined and had some limitations. However, it is mentioned that they called for a boycott of the Group Areas Act, but it was not effective in stopping its implementation because those who owned property were afraid that boycotting would make things worse for them.

In excerpt [3], it is mentioned that the Unity Movement used the principle of Non-collaboration and the weapon of boycott, with events such as festivals serving as opportunities to contradict historical fabrications. However, this does not clearly define their boycott policy.

Excerpt [4] discusses a specific instance where the Unity Movement called for a vote boycott, resulting in some people spoiling their ballots by writing "For Full Democratic Rights" across them instead of voting for the government-approved candidates.

Excerpt [13] describes how members of the Coloured Advisory Council were boycotted and ostracized when they accepted service on the CAC.

Therefore, while there seems to be inconsistency between excerpts regarding Unity Movement boycott policy, it is possible to conclude that the movement used various forms of non-cooperation as a weapon against those collaborating with the apartheid government.

As for other related events in excerpt [8], it is mentioned that an alliance of the Unity Movement and the Communist party disintegrated because the Party began pressing for the addition of two Liberals to the committee, which was defeated. However, as it is part of an 'alliance', it is hard to make a definitive answer about the policy.

It is however clear that there were some inconsistencies in the application and strategy of boycott tactics employed by Unity Movement, and it had its drawbacks (see [9] where collapse of anti-apartheid resistance movement was linked to their boycott policy).
