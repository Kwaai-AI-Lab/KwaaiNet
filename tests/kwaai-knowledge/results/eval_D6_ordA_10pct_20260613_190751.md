### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act, although not mentioned directly in an excerpt [1] but referenced in many as the driving force behind forced removals, was a 1950 law that "transformed the country into a patchwork of Black 'Coloured', Indian and White areas" [5]. This legislation aimed to separate people by race and enforce segregation under apartheid laws. As the sources indicate:

* The act led to the uprooting of communities and ways of life, particularly in District Six, where people were forced out and dispersed across Cape Flats [2].
* Those who owned property were faced with difficulties as a result; some ignored the injunction to boycott the Group Areas Board, while others tried to find loopholes in the law [3][4].
* The legislation created problems for individuals whose families had complex ethnic backgrounds, making classification tricky and leading to challenges for their everyday lives [11].

The effects of this Act were:

* Residents lost the municipal franchise because they lived in sub-economic houses [12].
* Group areas led to the disintegration of communities as people moved out and District Six began to die, starting in the 1950s, when shops closed and a "haemorrhaging was gradual but definite for years" [20].
* House prices plummeted after Coloured Areas were declared White, forcing owners to accept low offers from new buyers who knew their properties were undervalued by the Group Area Boards [18][20].

In summary, the Group Areas Act directly affected District Six and its residents. It enforced forced removals, broke up families with complex ethnic backgrounds, led to property disputes among property owners, and marked a significant event in the disintegration of this vibrant community under apartheid rule.
