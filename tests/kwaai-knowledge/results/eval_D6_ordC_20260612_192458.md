### q10 — Tell me about Kloof Nek.

Kloof Nek is a connecting saddle of land in Cape Town, South Africa that joins Table Mountain to Lion's Head [2]. It sprawls protectively on the eastern flank of the city with its head gazing inscrutably down on Table Bay [2].

To get to Kloof Nek, one would need to walk up from Adderley Street outside Fletchers & Cartwrights, a department store that sold everything from flan cakes to furniture, until reaching the bus terminus for the single-decker trackless tram [18]. When descending into the woodland dell of Kloof Nek, visitors are met with its beauty, making any initial reluctance or weariness evaporate [19].

Kloof Nek was a popular destination for people from all walks of life. Lovers, mountaineers, nature lovers, and ramblers could be found there, as well as "Bergies," which is the local slang for "mountain dwellers" [3]. In addition to these groups, many families took pleasure trips to Kloof Nek.

Kloof Nek was also a favorite spot for picnics. People would often bring baskets with food and enjoy eating while taking in the stunning views of Table Mountain and Lion's Head. It was not uncommon to see groups of friends or family members having impromptu gatherings or even holding small parties there.

In terms of transport, during that time, people could take a tram from Adderley Street or walk up through various routes, including Kloof Street until reaching the top. There were also some steep paths leading down into the valley below but were often used by experienced hikers

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act [17] was a law that transformed South Africa into a patchwork of Black “Coloured”, Indian, and White areas. It was part of the Apartheid legislation and the crowning piece of the Government's plan to create separate living areas for different racial groups [7].

Under this act, people had to be properly classified or "colour-coded" to determine where they could live [7]. The Group Areas Board worked on an age-old theory that people tend to anticipate the wishes of the authorities [17], and they provided a scale of compensation in cases where the seller had to settle for less than the Board's valuation, although this was generally a minimal amount [18].

The Group Areas Act affected District Six by creating a situation where residents were effectively disfranchised once they moved into sub-economic houses [9]. The act also led to the forced removals of the community from their homes. As the author notes, District Six began to die in the early fifties as shops started closing and the community scattered and disintegrated [20].

The Non-White group most aggrieved by the Group Areas Act was the South African Indians [14]. They felt that the law created a distinct disadvantage for them because they were compelled to live in their own restricted area outside the city. The complexity of families with members classified as both Indian and Malay created a vexing problem, as it was not accounted for in the plans to ensure white dominance [19].

The act also led to a situation where residents had to navigate bureaucratic obstacles, including the requirement that property owners sell at below market value to prospective White buyers. This further eroded the livelihoods of the community and ultimately led to their displacement.

Overall, the Group Areas Act played a significant role in shaping the experiences of the people living in District Six and was part of the larger Apartheid regime's efforts to control the lives of Black people in South Africa.
