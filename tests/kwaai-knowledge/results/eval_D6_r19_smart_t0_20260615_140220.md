### q05 — Who was J.M.H. Gool?

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act was a law passed in South Africa in 1950 that aimed to separate different racial groups into distinct areas. The act allowed the government to classify people according to their race and relocate them to designated areas, effectively forcing communities to move out of their homes and neighborhoods.

In the case of District Six, the Group Areas Act had a devastating impact on the community. According to [8], the Group Areas Act was "an obscene social experiment" that would devastate the lives of hundreds of thousands of unfranchised people in South Africa. The act led to the forced removals of residents from District Six, which was a thriving cosmopolitan area in Cape Town.

As mentioned in [13] and [14], the government built new townships on the outskirts of Cape Town, such as Bonteheuwel, Hanover Park, Mannenberg, Mitchell's Plain, Vanguard Estate, and Bishop Lavis, to entice people away from District Six. However, these townships were sub-economic and resulted in the residents losing their municipal franchise.

The Group Areas Act also affected the economic livelihood of the community. As mentioned in [16], the Indian population was concerned about their future if they were herded into one group area, as it would lead to a decline in their businesses and trade. The act effectively forced people to leave their homes, businesses, and communities behind.

The Group Areas Act had far-reaching consequences for District Six, leading to the displacement of thousands of residents, the destruction of homes and businesses, and the erasure of the community's cultural heritage.
