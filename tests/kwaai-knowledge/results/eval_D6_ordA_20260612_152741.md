### q11 — What was the Teachers League of South Africa (TLSA)?

The Teachers' League of South Africa (TLSA) was a non-European teachers' professional body that took a strong political stance against apartheid education policies [1]. They were members of various professions who were in the forefront of the struggle for full democratic rights for non-citizens in the land of their birth [5, 14]. One of its notable slogans was "Let Us Live for Our Children" and was mentioned to be a part of the Liberation Movement [3]. 

TLSA had conferences that served as important civic occasions, opened by the mayor with a civic banquet at the ratepayers' expense [2, 19]. It was also described as an organization in the forefront of the struggle for democracy [14] and had members like Alie Fataar, Edgar Maurice, Jane Gool, Ben Kies, Rev Gordon, Walter Parry, R.O.Dudley, Solly Edross, E.C Roberts, Abe Desmore, Rev Dan Wessels, Frank Landsman, and others who were considered leaders in the community [6].

The TLSA had an Educational Journal where teachers could receive training in political/educational composition and have their articles published. However, one needed to be called by the editor to enter this inner sanctum, and the speaker was Edgar Maurice, the Vice President of the TLSA [11].

### q14 — Where was District Six and what kind of place was it?

District Six was a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighborhood of Cape Town, South Africa [1]. It was home to the Rassool and Gool families and thousands of other residents before the apartheid government forcibly removed its population and demolished its homes under the Group Areas Act in the 1970s and 1980s.

Buitencingle (also spelled Buitensingel), which is an area within District Six, was at that time the outermost belt of Cape Town [5]. The area had a unique cultural flavor, with many eccentric characters, including people who made their living by performing on street corners and earning pennies from passersby [7].

District Six was known for being a cosmopolitan area where people from different cultures lived together. It was described as having "more life, more zest, more fire, more warmth and care than many other places one could mention" [18]. However, by the 1970s and 1980s, it had become a "black spot" in the Apartheid scheme of things, and its residents were forcibly removed under the Group Areas Act.

### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) was a South African political movement founded in 1943 [1] that united non-European political organisations opposed to apartheid and racial discrimination. It advocated for non-collaboration with apartheid institutions and was closely connected to the All African Convention and the Teachers League of South Africa [1].

The NEUM had its roots in the idea of non-European unity, which emerged in 1943 when the government attempted to impose segregation on "coloured" people through the Coloured Affairs Department. Although earlier attempts by Dr.Jabavu (noted in excerpt [17] and [20]) to create a non-European unity movement had failed due to historical circumstances not being ready for it, the idea gained momentum with the NEUM's founding [18].

The movement played a significant role in revamping the political scene, introducing the doctrine of non-collaboration and the boycott weapon. It also made efforts to "take a nation to school" by bringing people to a realisation that a programme-based struggle was needed to reject the deputations and appeals for justice at round table conferences [12].

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act [1, 4] was a piece of legislation in South Africa that allowed the government to forcibly remove people from their homes and relocate them to specific areas based on their racial classification. The act was designed to separate different racial groups into distinct residential areas.

In the case of District Six, the Group Areas Act had a devastating impact [1]. The area was a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighborhood in Cape Town, South Africa, that was home to the Rassool and Gool families and thousands of other residents. However, under the Act, the government declared that District Six would be reclassified as a "White" area, and its population, which consisted mostly of Coloured people, Indians, and Africans, would be forcibly removed to elsewhere.

The implementation of the Group Areas Act led to the forced removals of thousands of residents from District Six [9]. The community was scattered and disintegrated, leaving many people without homes or livelihoods. Many residents who owned property were forced to accept paltry offers for their properties, which were often undervalued by the Group Areas Board [2].

The effect on small traders in District Six was particularly severe [15]. The Act required them to relocate their businesses to a group area for Indians, but it was unclear how many shops such an area could sustain. As a result, they struggled to adapt to new regulations and restrictions imposed by the Group Areas Board.

The Group Areas Act also had a profound impact on the social fabric of District Six. It led to the erosion of community ties and the breakdown of social relationships among residents who were forced to leave their homes [20]. The Act legitimized poverty and oppression for the majority of South Africa's people, uprooting communities and ways of life.

Overall, the Group Areas Act was a key instrument of apartheid policy that aimed to create separate racial residential areas. Its implementation in District Six had severe consequences for the community, leading to forced removals, displacement, and social dislocation.
