### q06 — Tell me about Buitencingle.

Buitencingle was a 13-room mansion located on Buitencingle Street in District Six, Cape Town. It was the family home of the Gool family and served as a gathering place for prominent visitors including Gandhi and Dr. Abdullah Abdurahman [1]. The house boasted an expansive garden with fruit trees, which provided ample space for play and adventure for the children living there [3].

The author recalls spending time at Buitencingle, where he would climb trees, tell stories to his siblings, and explore the garden [3]. He also remembers the visits of Aboeta, a distant relative who had a tendency to get into trouble, but was occasionally charming and told tales of the family's history [8].

Buitencingle's location makes it an important part of District Six's geography. Perched on a hill, it overlooks Cape Town and serves as a boundary between the urban settlement of District Six and its neighbors [12]. The nearby suburb, Kloof Nek was at one time the bus terminus for the single-decker trackless tram that ran from Adderley Street outside Fletchers & Cartwrights, and on further into the town [12].

Buitencingle was also a place of social gatherings. It was not until the early years of settlement that many houses in this area were furnished with all their contents - in the photograph of Buitencingle you can see much empty space, the furniture being brought over from various parts of the city [9]. The house received numerous visitors and locals, including Gandhi who visited there on several occasions [18].

However, as the story progresses, it's mentioned that Buitencingle was eventually left to become a "dead shell" after the family abandoned it and went elsewhere in Cape Town [7]

### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) [1] was a South African political movement that united non-European political organisations opposed to apartheid and racial discrimination. It was founded in 1943 and advocated for non-collaboration with apartheid institutions. The NEUM aimed to unite the majority of oppressed people through politicising their non-political structures, such as sports bodies, churches, charities, mothers' unions, rate-payers, and civics [8]. 

According to source [10], it brought "the principle of non-collaboration, the refutation of “race”, and the ideal of a non-racial society" to the struggle for liberation.

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?
