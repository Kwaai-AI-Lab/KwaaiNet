The TLSA challenged racial discrimination in education and fought for equal opportunities for Coloured and non-European people [1].

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act [1] was apartheid legislation that forcibly divided South Africa into racially segregated residential and business zones. Under this Act, District Six was declared a White group area in 1966, leading to the forced removals of its entire non-White population – tens of thousands of families. Their homes were demolished and bulldozed, and residents were relocated to townships on the Cape Flats, far from the city [1].

The Act caused immense social disruption, destroying the vibrant multi-cultural community of District Six [1]. The apartheid government used it to implement racial segregation through forced removals across South Africa [1].

In District Six, the Group Areas Act led to a gradual but definite hemorrhaging of the area's population as shops in Hanover Street started closing, and the community scattered and disintegrated [14]. House prices plummeted when a Coloured Area was declared White, making it difficult for owners to sell their properties at a profit [14].

The Group Areas Act also affected the Indian community in South Africa. The Indian population of about half a million was largely concentrated in Natal, but there was a noticeable section who had achieved considerable wealth despite the odds against them – the merchant class [15]. However, this community was also severely impacted by the Group Areas Act, as they were forced to live in their own restricted area outside the city [16].

### q40 — What was the Unity Movement's boycott policy?

The Unity Movement's boycott policy was to refuse cooperation with apartheid institutions and those who participated in them [1]. This included boycotting elections, apartheid institutions, and individuals who took part in them. The boycott weapon was seen as a practical expression of non-collaboration, which was the fundamental political principle of the Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) [1].

The policy was enforced by the TLSA and NEF, and it was believed that a morally superior idea would overcome an inferior one, even if it meant temporary setbacks [4]. The boycott was seen as a powerful tool to challenge apartheid and bring about change.

However, there were instances where members of the Unity Movement did not follow the boycott policy. For example, some members appeared before the Group Areas Boards or sent their legal representatives to register their protest against the decision to have their areas reclassified [17].
