### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act, also known as the Prohibition of Mixed Marriage Act (1950), aimed to separate different racial groups into specific residential areas. This law had a devastating impact on the community in District Six.

According to sources [2], [4], and [14], the act legitimized poverty and oppression for the majority of South Africa's peoples, circumscribed where they could live – uprooting communities and ways of life. The Group Areas Act effectively transformed the country into a patchwork of Black “Coloured”, Indian, and White areas.

As stated in source [14], the Juggernaut of Apartheid was determined to eradicate any remaining rights of non-White oppressed groups. This act paved the way for further displacement and apartheid policies in South Africa.

Regarding District Six specifically, sources [2] and [17] describe how the community began to disintegrate due to the Group Areas Act. The once-thriving community's social fabric was severely damaged as residents were forcibly relocated from their homes into sub-economic housing schemes outside of the city. This resulted in the loss of municipal franchise for affected residents, leaving them effectively disfranchised.

The Group Areas Board’s valuation of properties often fell below market value, compelling Coloured owners to accept meager offers from White buyers (Source [20]). As houses became uninhabitable and resale values plummeted, entire communities were uprooted, leading to the eventual eradication of the vibrant District Six community.
