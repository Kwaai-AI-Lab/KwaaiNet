### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) was a South African political movement founded in 1943 that united non-European organizations opposed to apartheid and racial discrimination [1]. It played a significant role in the story, with its leaders and members closely related to the narrator's family [1].

The NEUM brought three key principles to the struggle for liberation: the principle of non-collaboration, the refutation of “race”, and the ideal of a non-racial society [12]. It also used the boycott as a weapon in the struggle against apartheid (13).

However, the movement was not without criticism. Victor Kessie regarded intellectuals who were part of the NEUM as having failed to make any contributions to literature by writing stories, novels, etc., seeing this as a significant shortcoming due to the system of Apartheid [7].

The NEUM achieved some successes, such as galvanizing non-white disenfranchised individuals towards programme-based struggles and taking control of the Teachers' League of South Africa after a fierce struggle [18]. However, its attempts were not without precedent, with Dr. Jabavu previously attempting to create a non-European unity movement three times prior [17].

The Non-European Unity Movement was closely related to other organizations such as the New Era Fellowship (NEF), and individuals like I.B. Tabata played important roles in the organization [4].

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act, as described in excerpts [6] and [18], was a law that aimed to separate different racial groups into distinct areas. The Act was part of the Apartheid legislation introduced by the South African government.

In the context of District Six, the act had devastating effects (excerpt [16]). Under this act, residents were forced out of District Six as it was declared "White" and was slated for redevelopment. This led to the displacement of many families, and their homes were either knocked down or taken over by white owners.

The law resulted in the inhabitants losing municipal voting rights due to their new addresses (see excerpt [10]) - effectively disfranchising them.

Many residents protested against this act (excerpt [15]), including those who sent letters and newspaper ads (excerpt [14]). Even some whites like Senator Edgar Brookes spoke out against it, although mostly they were ineffective in slowing the Apartheid regime (excerpt [15]).

The impact on the affected population was severe: hundreds of thousands lived through immense difficulties caused by this legislation. The government implemented this "obscene social experiment" to 'reorganize' society according to race.
