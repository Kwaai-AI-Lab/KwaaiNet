In source [18], Joe Rassool mentions that he began writing about Buitencingle as an effort to share his family's story with his children settled in the U.K., aiming to teach them about their roots.

### q14 — Where was District Six and what kind of place was it?

District Six was a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighborhood of Cape Town, South Africa. According to [1], it was home to the Rassool and Gool families and thousands of other residents before the apartheid government forcibly removed its population and demolished its homes under the Group Areas Act in the 1970s and 1980s.

The book also describes District Six as a place with "life, more zest, more fire, more warmth and care than many other places one could mention" ([2]). It was a neighborhood where people from different cultures and backgrounds lived together, and it had its own unique character and community. The author remembered walking away from it like Lot, without any regret, when it was eventually bulldozed out of existence due to the Apartheid government's policies.

The description of District Six in [11] and [20] also highlights its poor living conditions, with houses lacking ceilings or internal doors, and residents being subject to municipal franchise disadvantages. However, despite these difficulties, District Six remained a vibrant community until it was destroyed by the apartheid government.

### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) was a South African political movement founded in 1943 that united non-European political organisations opposed to apartheid and racial discrimination [1]. It advocated non-collaboration with apartheid institutions and was closely connected to the All African Convention and the Teachers League of South Africa [1].

According to some sources, the idea of non-European unity arose when the government foisted The Coloured Affairs Committee on the so-called Coloured people in 1943 [2]. Dr. Jabavu strove to create a non-European unity movement three times before its founding in 1943 [2].

The NEUM sought to unify different racial groups (including "Coloured," Indian, African and Malay/African) to resist the discriminatory policies of the apartheid regime [9]. They advocated for a non-racial society and adopted a policy of non-collaboration with the government.

The Unity Movement was instrumental in galvanizing the non-white disenfranchised to a realisation of the need for a programme-based struggle that rejected old tactics such as deputations and appeals for justice at round table conferences [9].

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The provided sources do not contain detailed information about the Group Areas Act, but they discuss its impact on District Six.

According to [2], the Group Areas Act caused "Apartheid" of District Six by declaring a Coloured Area white, thereby causing house prices to plummet. The owners were forced to accept low offers from White purchasers. This resulted in the community scattering and disintegrating.

[12] describes the Group Areas Act as the "crowning piece of the Government legislation" that transformed South Africa into a patchwork of Black, Coloured, Indian, and White areas with the help of the act called the lynch pin or Big Bertha of Apartheid weaponry. To enforce this plan, all people had to be properly classified (colour-coded).

[13] notes that District Six thrived in Victorian times as a community of freed slaves from America and the West Indies, but their expulsion at the turn of the century foreshadowed Apartheid evictions decades later.

The Group Areas Act had various consequences for District Six. It resulted in people being herded into one group area, causing concern about small traders' ability to make a living [16]. The act led to people seeking loopholes in the law, but these were often closed by the White-dominated legal profession. Many property owners ignored the injunction to boycott [14].

These sources demonstrate that the Group Areas Act caused significant disruption and displacement of communities in District Six, resulting from its enforcement by colour-coding its residents and causing them to be scattered across the Cape Flats as documented in [20].
