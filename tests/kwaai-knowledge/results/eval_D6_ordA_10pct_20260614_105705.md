### q05 — Who was J.M.H. Gool?

J.M.H. Gool, also known as Joosub Maulvi Hamid Gool, was a maternal grandfather of the author (Joe Rassool). He was a prosperous merchant from India who arrived at the Cape via Mauritius in 1884 [1]. ### q14 — Where was District Six and what kind of place was it?

According to sources [1] and [17], District Six was a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighbourhood of Cape Town, South Africa. It was located in the outermost belt of Cape Town, also referred to as "Buitencingle" ([4]).

District Six was described as a place with great life, zest, fire, warmth, and care ([2]). The community had a rich cultural and ethnic diversity, with many racial groups living together. It was considered a cosmopolitan area in the heart of Cape Town.

The place was also known for its makeshift homes, narrow streets, and lack of urban development. Many houses were without ceilings or internal doors, and there was limited street lighting ([11]).

District Six was a bustling community with many colourful characters and eccentrics who gave it a special flavour ([15]). However, like other African townships, it faced the threat of forced removals and eventual demolition under apartheid policies.

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act (1950) was a law passed in South Africa that aimed to separate different racial groups into distinct areas, with Whites being allocated the most favorable locations. The act allowed for the forced removal of Non-White people from their neighborhoods, particularly in urban areas like Cape Town.

In District Six, a thriving cosmopolitan area in Cape Town, the Group Areas Act did not specify its designation as a White neighborhood. However, it was reclassified under the act (Source: [13]), which had the effect of transforming many Non-White residents into "outsiders" (Source: [17]).

As a result, people who owned property in District Six, especially wealthier Indians and Whites, could profit from selling their properties to White buyers or the Group Areas Board (Sources: [2] and [15]). On the other hand, those who could not afford to buy sub-economic housing in areas like Bonteheuwel received low compensation for their homes in District Six as they were reclassified by the Group Areas Act. 

This effectively meant that lower-income Non-White residents of the neighborhood could no longer live there and those that lived already lost the right to a municipal franchise (Source: [9]). The Group Areas Act marked an irreversible shift toward a rigid ethnic zoning policy based on 'colour classification' in apartheid South Africa, contributing significantly to furthering segregation.

In essence, the law led to forced removals of inhabitants and destroyed communities like District Six which eventually fell prey to mass demolition and ultimately, to death by neglect – leading to what could be considered "destruction of an urban culture"
