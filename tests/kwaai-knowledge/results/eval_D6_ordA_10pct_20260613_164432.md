### q11 — What was the Teachers League of South Africa (TLSA)?

The Teachers’ League of South Africa (TLSA) was a non-European teachers' professional body that took a strong political stance against apartheid education policies. It was an important civic occasion, with annual conferences opened by the mayor and featuring social events, displays of eloquence on teaching as a profession, and opportunities for politicians to catch the eye of the president [2]. The TLSA allowed its members to receive training in the art of political/educational composition and publish their articles in the Journal of the Teachers’ League of South Africa, provided they were considered good writers and approved by the editor [8]. 

The organization was led by progressive and radical-minded teachers who wanted to challenge apartheid and improve education for non-whites. They advocated for full democratic rights for non-citizens and worked on strategies to collaborate with other anti-apartheid groups to prepare for the "eventuality" of increasing government restrictions and policies aimed at separating whites from "non-Whites" in all spheres of life [17, 18].

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The Group Areas Act was a piece of legislation passed in 1950 that aimed to classify people into different racial groups, which were then assigned specific areas in which they could live [6]. The law effectively created separate living spaces for different racial groups, with Whites having exclusive access to the most desirable and well-resourced areas.

In Cape Town, District Six was one of the areas affected by the Group Areas Act. The community was a vibrant mix of people from different ethnic backgrounds, including Indians, Malays, Coloureds, and others [13]. However, under the new laws, many of its residents were forced to relocate to other areas that were designated for their specific racial group.

The impact on District Six was devastating. According to the sources, "A potent source of misery occurred when a Coloured Area was declared White: the house prices plummeted as the new buyers were aware that the Coloured owners, who realized that the Group Areas Board's valuation of their properties was generally below the market value, were obliged to accept any paltry offer from prospective White purchasers" [2].

Many residents of District Six had to leave their homes and businesses behind. The area became a "stillborn death", where families were forcibly removed and scattered across other parts of Cape Town [8]. The once-thriving community was destroyed, leaving many people without basic amenities such as proper housing, sanitation, and healthcare.

The Group Areas Act also created complex problems for mixed-race families like the one described in the sources. For example, when some members of a family were classified as Indians and others as Malays, it created a "most vexing problem" due to the different requirements imposed by the Act [13].

### q39 — What was District Six like as a neighbourhood before the forced removals?

According to [1] and [17], District Six was a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighborhood of Cape Town, South Africa. It was home to the Rassool and Gool families and thousands of other residents [1]. Before the apartheid government's forced removals under the Group Areas Act in the 1970s and 1980s, the community of District Six had developed a rich history and culture [17].

As described by various sources:

* It was a thriving cosmopolitan area with diverse communities living together [17].
* The neighborhood was characterized as having "more life, more zest, more fire, more warmth and care" compared to other areas [18].
* District Six was known for its pulsing life, joy, and community spirit [18].

The district also had a history of politicking, activism, and resistance against apartheid.
