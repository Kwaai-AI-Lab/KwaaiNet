### q01 — What was the central argument of the Einstein-Szilard letter to President Roosevelt, and what action did it urge?

[3], [5], [6] The Einstein-Szilard letter urged that a new approach be taken in international relations due to the urgency of mutual confidence. 

It argued that the development of atomic power would necessitate the establishment of effective control measures, which would involve intricate technical and administrative problems. However, it did not explicitly state what these measures should be.

The letter also mentioned the need for watchfulness and, if necessary, quick action on the part of the Administration.

### q02 — What position did the Franck Report take regarding the use of the atomic bomb against Japan?

The Franck Report [6] states that a large number of scientists who have worked in this field won't clearly and unmistakably on record as to their opposition on moral grounds to the use of these bombs in the present phase of the war.

This report suggests that the use of nuclear bombs for an early, unannounced attack against Japan is inadvisable. The Franck Report argues that if the United States were the first to release this new means of indiscriminate destruction upon mankind, she would sacrifice public support throughout the world, precipitate the race of armaments, and prejudice the possibility of reaching an international agreement on the future control of such weapons.
