### q19 — What was the Non-European Unity Movement?

[1] states: The Non-European Unity Movement (NEUM) was a South African political movement founded in 1943 that united non-European political organisations opposed to apartheid and racial discrimination. It advocated non-collaboration with apartheid institutions and was closely connected to the All African Convention and the Teachers League of South Africa.

According to [15], the movement had a ten-point programme, which included:

* The full unfettered franchise - the right to elect and be elected to all the councils of the state.
* Free and equal education to the age of sixteen.

### q34 — What was the Group Areas Act and how did it affect District Six?

The provided sources do not mention the "Group Areas Act" explicitly, but they describe its effects on District Six. According to [2], the Group Areas Board's valuation of properties in District Six was generally below their market value, forcing owners to accept low offers from prospective White purchasers.

[14] explains that the Group Areas Act was a law passed by the Nationalist government in 1950 as part of its Apartheid legislation. The act aimed to transform South Africa into a patchwork of Black "Coloured", Indian, and White areas by classifying people according to their racial groups and forcing them to live in designated areas.

[14] states that the Act was the cornerstone or backbone (comparable to "Big Bertha" 61) of Apartheid legislation. It led to the forced removals of people from District Six to other townships, such as Bonteheuwel, Hanover Park, Mannenberg, Mitchell’s Plain, Vanguard Estate, and Bishop Lavis [13]. The Act resulted in the decimation of the vibrant community, destroying the lives of hundreds of thousands of non-White South Africans [19].

According to [6] and [18], the Group Areas Act affected small traders in District Six, as they were herded into one group area. This would lead to job losses and affect their livelihoods.

Sources also mention that the Indian population was particularly adversely affected by the Group Areas Act. As of 1960, according to [16] nearly half a million South African Indians lived mainly in Natal (90% were working-class or poor peasants), but many had achieved some wealth and prosperity despite facing challenges. There was still considerable social prejudice against them, as their "advantages" (over Whites) made Whites feel that Indians could prosper on less [16].

[2] indicates a slow decline of business in District Six starting in the early 1950s, which ultimately contributed to its dispersal.

The Group Areas Act effectively demolished the non-apartheid legacy district of District Six, causing loss of identity and culture for thousands of South Africans [20].

### q39 — What was District Six like as a neighbourhood before the forced removals?

[1] describes District Six as "a vibrant, multi-cultural inner-city neighbourhood of Cape Town, South Africa. It was home to the Rassool and Gool families and thousands of other residents before the apartheid government forcibly removed its population and demolished its homes under the Group Areas Act in the 1970s and 1980s."

The book describes District Six as a "pulsing life" with "more life, more zest, more fire, more warmth and care than many other places one could mention." (Source: [2])

It was described as a neighborhood where "to rekindle the memory of District Six in its heyday, and to provide a realistic picture to their children... I would dearly like to take you on a walk-through streets that once buzzed with the joy of life," (Source: [2]).

The neighborhood had a mix of cultures and was home to many people from different backgrounds. The book describes it as a place where "Apartheid legitimized poverty and oppression for the majority of its peoples, circumscribed where they could live – uprooting communities and ways of life," (Source: [5])

It is also mentioned that before the forced removals, District Six was thriving, and people lived in close-knit communities. The book states "There are many things to learn from this book..." including "the rich tapestry of the lives of the people who made District Six such a distinctive part of the city," (Source: [5]).

Overall, District Six is described as a vibrant and multicultural neighborhood with a strong sense of community before the forced removals.
